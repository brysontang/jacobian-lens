The Ghost-Token Lens

Running Anthropic's Jacobian lens backward: instead of asking what a residual-stream state is disposed to say, ask what the input would have had to be to produce it.

Qwen3-0.6B · 28 layers Kℓ = E[∂hℓ/∂emb], 24 WikiText prompts eval: 8 held-out prompts 2026-07-07

The forward lens reads unembed(J·h) — a next-token disposition. Here we fit the mirror-image Jacobian K = E[∂h/∂emb] and decode a residual vector h by enumerating the vocabulary: score each token by ⟨h, K·e_v⟩ − ½‖K·e_v‖², i.e. find the input embedding whose forward image under the average linearized dynamics lands nearest to h. No matrix inversion, no SVD cutoff — the readout is well-posed by construction. The result is a ghost input: not a causal reconstruction, but the "as-if context" the state is behaving as though it received.

The two curves cross between layers 20 and 23 — roughly 75–80% of the way through the network. Before that point the residual stream is still, in a linear-readout sense, about its input; after it, it's about the output. The baseline (matching h directly against raw embeddings, no Jacobian) sits at ~0% with median ranks in the tens of thousands at every layer: the Jacobian is carrying all of the signal, not the embedding geometry.

Curious wrinkle: the curve is non-monotonic — a dip at layer 8 (49%) before recovering to 65–74% at layers 11–17. Consistent with an early "detokenization" phase that temporarily moves the state off the input manifold before mid-network processing settles it back.

2  /  Nothing moves. It dissolves in place.

The original question: after self-attention mixes tokens together, is the model rearranging its sentence — does position p start holding a token from elsewhere in the prompt? We classify each position's ghost top-1 as self (the token that's actually there), moved (a token from elsewhere in the prompt), or novel (not in the prompt at all).

self — ghost is the actual token here moved — a token from elsewhere in the prompt novel — a token not in the prompt

"Moved" never exceeds 4% at any layer, in either prompt. The answer to the rearrangement question is a clean no — at least under the averaged linear readout, the residual stream never impersonates a shuffled version of its context. When a position stops holding its own token, it doesn't pick up a neighbor's; it drifts to something outside the prompt entirely. Identity is lost by dissolution, not permutation.

The repo's ASCII-face prompt, reconstructed from each layer's ghost top-1 tokens. The structural tokens — bars, parens, the ^ nose — are recovered essentially verbatim through layer 20 (the ^ stays a ^; it never becomes "nose"). Then, over three layers, the whole drawing dissolves into an attractor soup: .INSTANCE, PasswordEncoder, zero-width spaces.

5  /  Follow-ups: the rigor pass

Is the handoff a per-token trigger? No. On 888 held-out positions, how long a position holds its input token does not predict when it converges on its prediction (partial correlation +0.06 after controlling for word-continuation status and prediction confidence). At matched layers the coupling is negative: positions still holding their token are also closer to converged — both readouts succeed or fail together, suggesting a shared per-position linearization-quality factor. The handoff is a network-wide regime change in the L20–23 band, not a chain of individual releases.

Each dot is one held-out position. Down-and-left = input token still held and prediction nearly converged. The downward tilt at L14–L20 is the negative coupling: stability and predictive convergence travel together. By L23 the input axis has collapsed (the cloud slides right) and the coupling dissolves with it.

Do positions jump between the walls, or slide? Tracing every position through all nine sampled layers: input-identity release is a snap — 97% of wall-to-wall trips (held → dissolved) complete within a six-layer window, 52% within three, with departures synchronized at layers 17–20. Output convergence is a climb — transits spread over three to fifteen layers, with departures staggered from layer 2 onward. And the ordering is close to law-like: at 78% of positions the prediction locks in before or at the same layer as the identity releases (output first 50%, same layer 28%, input first 22%). The migration hugs the bottom of the L20 panel because the model doesn't let go of a token until it has mostly decided what to say.

Is deep-layer ghost "junk" meaningful? Causally, yes — gradedly. Take the prompt, replace a position's actual input token with its L23 ghost top-1 (the edit is to the input text, then a full rerun), and the next-token prediction at that position survives:

The gradient matters: near-miss ghosts (marks → marking) are working substitutes the model accepts; fully dissolved junk barely beats control. By L23 the state has rotated off the input manifold — it still leans toward its output (ghosts drift toward "the input that would have caused this prediction"), but the information no longer compresses into any single vocabulary token.

Caveats & next

  • Not causal. K is an average Jacobian over 24 WikiText prompts; the ghost input is the nearest preimage under mean linearized dynamics, not a claim about what the network actually received.
  • One model, one seed. Qwen3-0.6B, layers {2,5,…,26}. Qwen3.5-2B was benchmarked and rejected (fp16 gradients NaN past layer 20 on its hybrid linear-attention fallback; >24 h fit on this machine).
  • Deep-layer dissolution is metric-robust. A single-prompt probe confirmed l2, dot, and cosine readouts all agree at layer 26 — the identity loss is real, not an artifact of the −½‖Ke_v‖² norm term.
  • Next: offset-resolved KΔ — one matrix per lookback distance, K_Δ = E[∂h_l[p+Δ]/∂emb[p]], giving each position a ghost profile over distance: "what, and from where." This is the cross-position instrument that sections 2 and 5 lacked — fit in progress.
